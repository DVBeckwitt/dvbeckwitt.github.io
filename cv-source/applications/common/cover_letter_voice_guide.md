# Cover Letter Voice Guide

Use this guide with `cover_letter_base_format.md` and the job-family cover-letter formats. It captures David's preferred cover-letter voice from his Beamport Engineer revision.

## Preferred voice

The preferred cover-letter voice is direct, first-person, concrete, and modestly confident. The letter should sound like a scientist explaining why the role is close to work he already does, not like a generic applicant trying to sound broadly impressive.

Use a composed, practical tone. Let the evidence carry the confidence. Prefer clear sentences such as `I am applying for...`, `My graduate research has centered on...`, `That experience is directly relevant to...`, `I also bring...`, and `The role also depends on...`.

Do not hide real gaps. When a role uses an adjacent method or domain, name the boundary once in neutral language and then return to the transferable evidence. Example pattern: `That experience is directly relevant to [role need], even though my operational background is [direct evidence] rather than [posting-specific method].`

## Preferred structure

1. Opening: state the role and employer, give David's current identity, and explain why the role is close to his current work or duties.
2. Primary proof: describe the strongest technical evidence in plain first person. Use concrete methods, instruments, data types, and analysis outputs.
3. Secondary proof: add software, instrumentation, hardware-adjacent work, teaching, or communication only when it supports the posting.
4. Role-context proof: connect the evidence to the people or systems the role supports, such as scientists, students, users, instruments, data pipelines, customers, or classrooms.
5. Close: use one direct sentence about discussing how his background can support the team.

## Sentence-level rules

Use first person without apology. `I operate`, `I build`, `I have explained`, and `I would bring` are appropriate when supported by evidence.

Keep technical lists controlled. A list of methods or tools is acceptable when it is part of a concrete sentence and the role needs those details. Do not list every tool in every paragraph.

Use employer-specific context without flattery. Explain why the role is close to David's work, who the role supports, or what instruments, data, students, users, or problems are involved.

Prefer `with` over `at` when the letter is addressed to a program, reactor, lab, department, or unit and the phrasing sounds more natural: `with the University of Missouri Research Reactor`.

Avoid brand-like openings such as `I am excited to leverage`, `I am thrilled by your mission`, or `My passion for innovation makes me an ideal fit`.

## Recommended close

Use a restrained close:

`I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my [specific evidence], [specific evidence], and [specific evidence] background can support [team, program, or role].`

Do not end with a broad promise to create impact, transform the organization, or bring a unique passion.

## Beamport-style calibration example

This is a style reference, not a template to copy word for word:

`I am applying for the [role] position with [organization]. I am a Physics Ph.D. candidate at the University of Missouri, expected July 2026, specializing in [specific technical areas]. [Organization]'s role is close to the work and duties I already perform: [concrete overlap].`

`My graduate research has centered on [core work]. I [operate/build/analyze] [specific instruments, models, or data]. That experience is directly relevant to [role need], even though my operational background is [direct experience] rather than [adjacent posting requirement].`
