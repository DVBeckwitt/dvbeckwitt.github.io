## Core Approach

Physics often appears to pre-med and engineering students as an impersonal stepping-stone course. I work against that perception by combining student-centered instruction with explicit expectations, active learning, and sustained rapport. Significant learning is more likely when students believe the instructor values their effort, their context, and their questions.

This approach does not mean lowering standards. It means making the route to those standards visible. Students should know what they are expected to do, how their progress will be measured, which activities build the required skills, and how feedback will help them improve.

## Small Groups and Tutoring

One-on-one tutoring sessions and small classes of 15 to 25 students taught me that learning is social and contextual. I have worked with students from nursing, pre-medical programs, physics, engineering, and the arts. In these settings, informal rapport often unlocks engagement before a formal explanation can succeed.

Reluctant learners become more receptive when they sense that their instructor values them as people. That relationship can take time away from content delivery in the short term, but it often produces deeper engagement, stronger persistence, and better retention of the physics.

## Large Courses

Scaling rapport to classes with more than 100 students requires deliberate design. In large sections, I use lab meetings and early-course surveys to learn student backgrounds, build name recognition, and create positive low-stakes interactions. I combine personal gestures, such as approachable office hours, with structured mechanisms such as anonymous polling, think-pair-share, and discussion boards.

These tools encourage participation and reveal misconceptions without requiring every student to speak publicly. They also let me adjust the balance between connection, content coverage, and assessment based on the goals of the course.

## Active Learning and Honors Sections

In honors sections of 25 to 30 students, I adopted a flipped-classroom model under my advisor's mentorship. Preparatory videos or readings introduce core ideas before class. Class time then focuses on collaborative problem solving, lab explorations, concise derivations, and targeted clarification.

Short quizzes and brief recaps support preparation. Inquiry-based tutorials, group projects, demonstration days, and simulations give students room to test ideas, explore parameter changes, and move beyond the narrow conditions of a standard lab handout.

## Assessment and Course Design

Clarity and explicit expectations guide every lesson plan. I use four questions to align instruction with assessment.

| Planning question | Purpose |
|:--|:--|
| What should students be able to do by the end? | Define the outcome. |
| How will I measure mastery? | Identify the evidence. |
| Which tasks build the required skill? | Select the activities. |
| How will I collect timely assessment data? | Plan the feedback loop. |
{:.stretch-table}

This framework keeps each class tied to observable student progress. It also supports iterative course improvement, since weak evidence, unclear activities, or delayed feedback can be identified and adjusted.

## Teaching as Service

Teaching is inseparable from service. I have participated in demonstration days for local schools and collaborated with public-facing science programs, including observatory and outreach activities. These events reinforce that teaching deepens learning while connecting campus work to the broader community.

Outreach requires time, logistics, and coordination. The benefit is that students see physics as a public practice, not only as a classroom subject. That connection can strengthen purpose, civic responsibility, and retention.

## Future Development

### Peer Instruction

I will continue forming peer-instruction groups early in the semester. Stable groups give students time to develop trust, explain reasoning, and clarify misconceptions. The risk is that long-standing groups can develop inequitable dynamics. To reduce that risk, I will monitor participation, rotate groups when needed, and cold-call groups rather than individuals so responsibility is shared.

### Design Your Own Problem

I plan to expand a consistent Design Your Own Problem component. Students will author and solve an original problem tied to each unit. This gives them space to connect physics with their backgrounds and interests while reinforcing problem construction, modeling assumptions, and solution checking.

Some students may freeze when given an open-ended task. I will support them with examples, constraints, and collaborative brainstorming while preserving the creative responsibility of the assignment.

### Predict, Observe, Explain Demonstrations

Predict-Observe-Explain demonstrations will remain central to lectures. Students predict an outcome, observe a live or simulated experiment, and then explain agreement or discrepancy. This format prompts curiosity and directly exposes misconceptions.

Preparation requires time and materials. To keep demonstrations sustainable, I will build reusable kits, incorporate student-led demonstrations, and use simple household items when they can support the same conceptual goal.

### Context-Driven Examples

At the start of each course, I will continue polling students about their majors and interests. I will use that information more deliberately to tailor examples in homework, lectures, and demonstrations. Connecting physics to biology, medicine, engineering, education, art, and public life helps students see relevance beyond the exam.

The risk is that tailored examples can unintentionally reinforce stereotypes about majors. I will vary contexts across the term and invite students to propose examples themselves.

### Community Involvement

I will continue expanding community involvement by collaborating with department colleagues, local schools, and science centers for public demonstration nights and student volunteer opportunities. This goal depends on collaboration and existing programs, which keeps the workload sustainable while maintaining educational value.

## Closing Statement

I see teaching as a dialogue. I bring the physics curriculum, but students bring experiences that shape how the material is understood and used. My objectives are to teach core concepts, help students apply physics in their future work, and model the humility required to keep improving.

A strong teaching style should iterate like the science we aim to teach: carefully, deliberately, and in response to evidence.
