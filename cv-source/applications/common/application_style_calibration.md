# Application Style Calibration

Use this guide with `AGENTS.md`, `resume_base_format.md`, `cover_letter_base_format.md`, and the job-family templates. It captures David's current preferred style from the edited MURR Instrumentation & Control Technician I resume and cover letter.

## Overall style

The default application style is practical, concrete, and understated. It should read like a careful technical applicant explaining relevant evidence, not like an academic CV compressed into one page and not like a polished job-search template.

Prefer:

- Simple evidence before broad fit statements.
- Practical instrumentation, diagnostics, measurement validation, documentation, and troubleshooting language when relevant.
- Shorter sentences that connect observed behavior, expected behavior, and physical or technical context.
- Candid adjacent-fit language for requirements David has not directly done.
- Controlled employer context, not long restatements of the posting.

Avoid:

- A target-role line in the resume header.
- Dense academic detail unless the role needs it.
- Long lists of structural parameters, conference names, awards, or service roles in one-page industry resumes.
- Overly abstract phrases such as `instrument-user perspective` when a more concrete phrase works.
- Template phrases such as `strong fit`, `main transition`, `maps well`, `uniquely qualified`, or `leverage my skills`.

## Resume style

For one-page technical, instrumentation, facility, metrology-adjacent, analyst, and nonacademic resumes, use uppercase plain section headings by default:

`SUMMARY`

`CORE SKILLS`

`TECHNICAL EXPERIENCE`

`APPLIED RESEARCH TOOLS`, `PROJECTS`, or another role-specific tools section

`TEACHING AND TECHNICAL COMMUNICATION`, when teaching, tutoring, presentations, or workshops help the role

`EDUCATION`

Use a compact two-part summary when the role is practical or technical:

1. First sentence: current identity plus the most relevant hands-on evidence.
2. Second sentence or short paragraph: strengths framed as habits, such as careful instrument care, measurement validation, documentation, and troubleshooting by comparing observed behavior with expected physical behavior.

Default technical summary frame:

`Physics Ph.D. candidate at the University of Missouri, expected July 2026, with hands-on experience [direct evidence], [secondary evidence], and [software or workflow evidence].`

`Strengths are experience with [practical habit], [validation habit], [documentation habit], and troubleshooting by comparing observed system behavior with expected physical or technical behavior.`

For resumes, do not default to first person. Use a sentence-fragment or third-person profile unless David specifically asks for first person or the transition would otherwise sound artificial.

Use `CORE SKILLS` as label-led lines instead of generic bullet groups when possible:

`Instrumentation and lab systems: ...`

`Measurement and diagnostics: ...`

`Software and data: ...`

`Communication: ...`

Write bullets that are concrete but not over-dense. For practical technical roles, prefer language like:

- `Operate and maintain ... supporting stable data collection and reproducible measurements.`
- `Build Python-based ... workflows that convert, inspect, simulate, and analyze ... data.`
- `Diagnose unexpected ... behavior using detector geometry, physical simulation, experimental context, and model-data comparisons before drawing conclusions.`
- `Use documentation, code repositories, and validation planning to keep analysis workflows reproducible and reviewable.`

Omit awards, full presentation lists, publication sections, and detailed service leadership from one-page technical resumes unless the posting makes them relevant. Teaching and tutoring can stay when the role values communication, training, collaboration, or explanation.

Education should be compact. Bullet education lines are acceptable on one-page resumes.

## Cover letter style

Use a formal but simple letter block when information is available:

`June 15, 2026`

`Hiring Committee`

`[Department]`

`[Employer]`

`[City, State]`

`Re: [Exact role title]`

Start with identity and direct evidence:

`I am a Physics Ph.D. candidate at the University of Missouri, expected July 2026, with hands-on experience [direct evidence].`

Then connect to the role using concrete duties:

`My work experience is closely aligned with what this position demands involving [duty], [duty], [duty], and [duty].`

Use four or five short paragraphs for adjacent technical roles:

1. Identity and role alignment.
2. Current direct evidence.
3. Earlier or secondary evidence plus explicit boundary.
4. Employer environment and contribution.
5. Restrained close.

Preferred adjacent-fit language:

`That experience is adjacent rather than direct [target-domain work]. Those would be training areas for me. I do bring a base in [supported evidence], [supported evidence], and [supported evidence].`

Use `training areas for me` when the posting includes requirements such as PLC programming, reactor instrumentation, production quality systems, regulated calibration ownership, direct beamline work, direct sales ownership, or formal instructor-of-record experience that David does not have.

Prefer concrete contribution language:

`I would bring a technical background in [evidence], [evidence], [evidence], and communication while building the [role-specific] skills required by the facility.`

Close with:

`I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my [evidence], [evidence], and [evidence] background can support [team or program].`

## Compression rules

When space is tight, remove in this order:

1. Awards that are not directly relevant.
2. Full conference or presentation lists.
3. Service leadership that does not support the role.
4. Long structural-parameter lists.
5. Publications for nonacademic roles.
6. Duplicated claims about communication or documentation.

Keep the claim that best explains the role fit, even if it is less academically impressive.
