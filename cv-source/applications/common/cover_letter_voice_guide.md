# Cover Letter Voice Guide

Use this guide with `cover_letter_base_format.md` and the job-family cover-letter formats. It captures David's current preferred voice for job application materials.

## Preferred voice

Write as David would explain his background to a technical hiring manager. The tone should be direct, modest, analytical, first-person, and specific. Let concrete evidence carry the confidence.

Prefer claims about tools used, systems maintained, problems debugged, repositories owned, checks run, documentation written, instruments operated, students taught, and outputs produced. Avoid broad polish that makes research, teaching, or QA-adjacent work sound like direct industry experience when the evidence does not support that.

## Preferred structure

1. Opening: start with David's current identity and directly relevant experience, then name the role and employer naturally.
2. Primary proof: describe one direct project or work area that maps to the job.
3. Secondary proof: use graduate research, software, instrumentation, teaching, documentation, or communication to support broader technical credibility.
4. Transition and close: when the role is a career transition, name the transition candidly and close with what David is prepared to apply.

## Sentence-level rules

Use first person without apology when the evidence supports it: `I build`, `I maintain`, `I operate`, `I test`, `I document`, `I have not held a formal [job title] title, but...`.

Use moderate confidence. `I believe that experience maps well to...` is usually better than `this makes me an ideal candidate`.

Keep technical lists controlled. A list of methods or tools is useful only when it clarifies the role fit.

Use employer-specific context without flattery. One specific company or institution sentence is usually enough.

## Transition language

For transition roles, be candid about the gap:

`The main transition for me would be from [current domain] into [target domain].`

`I have not held a formal [job title] title, but I do bring the core habits [employer] is asking for: [supported habits].`

Then return to evidence: Python automation, validation scripts, reproducible workflows, automated quality checks, detector-data troubleshooting, technical documentation, simulation, modeling, expected-output comparisons, or analytical debugging.

## Language to avoid

Avoid these patterns unless David explicitly asks for a different style:

- `I am applying for the [job title] position...`
- `My strongest direct evidence is...`
- `The role is close to work I already do...`
- `research background built around...`
- `uniquely qualified`
- `proven track record`
- `passionate about`
- `excited to bring`
- `leverage my skills`
- `dynamic team`
- `strong fit`
- `hit the ground running`
- broad claims that imply direct industry experience when the evidence is research, tooling, teaching, or QA-adjacent experience

## Recommended close

Use a restrained close that names what David is prepared to apply:

`I am prepared to apply those habits in [role context], with the main transition being from [current domain] into [target domain].`

`I would welcome the chance to discuss how my [specific evidence], [specific evidence], and [specific evidence] can support [team, program, or role].`

Do not end with a broad promise to transform the organization or bring unique passion.
