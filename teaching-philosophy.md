## Overview

My teaching begins with clarity. Students should know what they are learning, why it matters, how their work will be evaluated, and how each activity helps them improve. Clear expectations make rigor more accessible without lowering the standard.

I pair that structure with active practice. In tutoring, small honors sections, large introductory courses, and outreach settings, I use questions, prediction, peer discussion, demonstrations, and guided problem solving to make students test their ideas before an exam tests them. Physics becomes more durable when students explain, revise, and apply concepts in public enough to learn from mistakes.

I also treat rapport as an instructional tool. Students bring different goals to physics: medicine, engineering, education, research, art, and everyday technical judgment. I try to learn those contexts early, then use examples and feedback that make the course feel accountable to the people in the room.

Assessment should support that process. I prefer feedback that identifies the next useful step, not only the error. Rubrics, polling, office hours, and low-stakes checks help me see where explanations fail and where students need another route into the same idea.

My long-term goal is a course environment that is organized, demanding, and humane. Students should leave with stronger physics skills, more confidence using quantitative reasoning, and a clearer sense that scientific understanding is built through evidence, revision, and collaboration.
