I ask what can be learned from an X-ray detector image before it is collapsed into a one-dimensional curve.
{:.lead}

Layered van der Waals films often sit between crystal and powder. Their layer normals stay aligned, while in-plane orientation, mosaic spread, and stacking sequence remain partly disordered. That mixed order is visible on the detector.

## Dissertation

_Investigating Disorder in van der Waals Thin Films_ develops forward models for area-detector X-ray diffraction and GIWAXS. The models keep experimental geometry, detector calibration, beam footprint, absorption, refraction, structure factors, mosaicity, and stacking disorder in one calculation.

Ordered Bi<sub>2</sub>Te<sub>3</sub> and Bi<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> films provide the clean geometry. CVD-grown PbI<sub>2</sub> films provide the disorder-sensitive case, where diffuse intensity along Q<sub>z</sub> is used to estimate stacking fault behavior.

## Core questions

| Question | Methods |
|:--|:--|
| Which detector features come from geometry? | WAXS, detector calibration, forward modeling |
| Which features come from orientation and mosaic spread? | Area-detector diffraction, oriented-powder simulation |
| Which features reveal stacking disorder? | GIWAXS, diffuse scattering, PbI<sub>2</sub> growth control |
| Which images can be screened automatically? | Simulated GIWAXS, PyTorch, convolutional networks |
