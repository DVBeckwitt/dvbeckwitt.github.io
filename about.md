I am David Beckwitt, a Physics Ph.D. candidate at the University of Missouri studying disorder in van der Waals thin films. My work combines quantitative X-ray scattering, custom diffraction instrumentation, thin-film materials characterization, Python-based modeling, and machine-learning-assisted structure analysis.

## Current Work

As a Graduate Research Assistant in Dr. Paul Miceli's group, I operate and maintain custom-built in-house X-ray diffraction and reflectivity systems for materials characterization. My dissertation, _Investigating Disorder in van der Waals Thin Films_, develops quantitative methods for connecting detector images to thin-film structure, orientation, and disorder.

A central part of this work is a Python-based GIWAXS modeling framework for extracting site occupancies, anisotropic Debye-Waller factors, mosaicity, and geometric parameters from scattering data. I also model diffuse scattering from stacking faults, quantify defect densities, grow phase-controlled PbI<sub>2</sub> films by chemical vapor deposition, and train PyTorch convolutional neural networks on simulated GIWAXS data for automated structure analysis.

## Applied Research Tools

- [2D_Mosaic_Sim](https://github.com/DVBeckwitt/2D_Mosaic_Sim): X-ray diffraction simulation tools for visualizing diffraction patterns from materials with specified crystal orientations.
- [ra_sim](https://github.com/DVBeckwitt/ra_sim): simulation and analysis software for diffraction data from R-Axis IV++ detectors.
- [OSC_Reader](https://github.com/DVBeckwitt/OSC_Reader): a detector-data converter for inspecting proprietary detector files without commercial software.

## Teaching and Outreach

Teaching is a central part of my academic work. I have tutored undergraduate physics at the University of Missouri, taught and assisted physics courses with enrollments from 15 to more than 200 students, and supported introductory mechanics, electricity and magnetism, and classical mechanics.

My teaching emphasizes clear expectations, student-centered support, active learning, collaborative problem solving, inquiry-based tutorials, demonstrations, simulations, and peer instruction. I have also contributed to public STEM outreach through MU Extension eclipse events, STEM Cubs, Moberly Correctional Center, Columbia Young Scientist Expo, high school physics demonstrations, and student mentoring.

## Background

I earned an M.S. in Physics from the University of Missouri in 2022 and a B.S. in Physics from Missouri State University in 2020, with minors in Mathematics and Chemistry. Before my current graduate research, I worked on graphene and thin-film materials through the NASA Space Consortium, designed and built a pulsed laser deposition system at Missouri State University, and developed biomedical testing software and hardware automation as an R&D intern at Dynatek Labs.
